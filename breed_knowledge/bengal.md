# Bengal (Cat)

## Exercise Needs
- Duration: 30–60 minutes of active play per day
- Frequency: Daily (multiple sessions)
- Intensity: High for a cat — running, jumping, fetching, puzzle feeders
- Most athletic domestic cat breed; needs significant physical and mental stimulation

## Common Health Issues
- Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM): labored breathing = emergency
- Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA): night blindness (genetic in Bengals)
- Tritrichomonas foetus: chronic diarrhea — common in Bengals
- Flat-chested kitten syndrome (FKS): affects young kittens, breathing difficulties
- Warning signs: open-mouth breathing = emergency; persistent loose stools, sudden vision problems

## Dietary Guidelines
- Adult: high-protein diet; ½–¾ cup dry or 3 oz wet, 2 meals daily
- Raw or wet food preferred — Bengals tend toward digestive sensitivity
- Senior (10+): senior high-protein formula with kidney monitoring
- Fresh running water essential (cat fountain strongly recommended)
- Restrictions: no onions, garlic, grapes, chocolate, xylitol

## Grooming Requirements
- Brushing: weekly (short, dense coat is low-maintenance)
- Bathing: rarely needed; many Bengals enjoy water
- Nail trimming: every 2–3 weeks (active scratchers — provide many scratching posts)
- Ear checks: monthly
- Teeth brushing: 2–3 times per week

## Temperament and Training Notes
- Extremely intelligent, active, and curious — can learn leash walking, fetch, and complex tricks
- Needs mental stimulation as much as physical — puzzle feeders, clicker training
- Can be destructive if bored; not suited for owners away long hours
- Vocal and demanding; forms strong bonds
- Compatible with children and dog-friendly households

## Age-Specific Care
- Kitten (0–1 year): 3–4 small meals/day, kitten-proof home (excellent climbers/jumpers)
- Adult (1–10 years): multiple daily play sessions, annual vet, high-protein diet
- Senior (10+ years): bi-annual vet, HCM screening, gentler play, monitor digestive health

# Maine Coon (Cat)

## Exercise Needs
- Duration: 20–30 minutes of active play per day
- Frequency: Daily
- Intensity: Moderate — interactive wand toys, fetch, climbing structures
- One of the most dog-like cat breeds: may follow owners around and enjoy walks on leash

## Common Health Issues
- Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM): coughing, labored breathing, sudden weakness — vet emergency
- Hip dysplasia: limping, reluctance to jump
- Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA): muscle weakness, wobbly gait (genetic)
- Polycystic kidney disease (PKD): increased thirst/urination, weight loss
- Warning signs: open-mouth breathing in cats is always an emergency; sudden hind-leg weakness

## Dietary Guidelines
- Adult: ½–¾ cup dry kibble or 2–3 oz wet food, 2 meals daily; large breed needs more protein
- Slow growth breed — don't rush to adult food before 18 months
- Senior (10+): senior formula with kidney support
- Restrictions: no onions, garlic, grapes, raisins, chocolate, xylitol
- Fresh water always available; Maine Coons love running water (consider a fountain)

## Grooming Requirements
- Brushing: 2–3 times per week to prevent matting (semi-long coat)
- Bathing: every 4–6 weeks if needed
- Ear checks: weekly
- Nail trimming: every 2–3 weeks
- Teeth brushing: 2–3 times per week

## Temperament and Training Notes
- Highly intelligent, social, and playful well into adulthood
- Can be trained to walk on leash, play fetch, and learn commands
- Gentle and tolerant — good with children, dogs, and other cats
- Vocal with soft chirps and trills; not typically a loud meower
- Needs vertical space — tall cat trees essential

## Age-Specific Care
- Kitten (0–18 months): 3–4 small meals/day, kitten formula, socialization
- Adult (18 months–10 years): interactive play daily, annual vet, dental care
- Senior (10+ years): bi-annual vet, HCM screening, kidney bloodwork, softer resting spots
